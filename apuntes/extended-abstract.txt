# Texto en español
Este trabajo aborda la simulación de los denominados NeuroStrikes (ataques de radiación electromagnética de alta potencia y baja frecuencia) sobre modelos realistas del hipocampo humano, con el propósito de evaluar sus efectos sobre los procesos de consolidación de la memoria y determinar posibles daños neuronales. Partiendo de la creciente preocupación que suscitan las interfaces cerebro computadora (los BCI) y el uso de pulsos electromagnéticos de alta potencia en el contexto de la llamada Guerra Cognitiva (en la que se inscriben episodios como el ``Síndrome de La Habana'', el cuál afectó a un gran número de personas), esta investigación busca replicar y ampliar las aportaciones de la tesis de Lennart Töllke (2024), proporcionando nuevas métricas, métodos de ataque y un análisis estadístico robusto.

En primer lugar, tras una revisión exhaustiva de la literatura y de trabajos fundamentales en neurociencia, BCIs y ciberataques neuronales, se seleccionó como plataforma de simulación el entorno Brian2. Se hizo esto para aprovechar un modelo detallado del hipocampo y la corteza entorrinal desarrollado y validado por Aussel et al. (2018, 2022). Además, este modelo es el que usa Töllke en su trabajo, de forma que no se ha tenido que implementar un simulador desde cero, sino que se ha podido aprovechar el código de Töllke para realizar los experimentos. Este modelo, que incorpora más de treinta mil neuronas organizadas en regiones CA3, CA1 y giro dentado, emplea archivos EEG reales registrados durante fases de sueño como estímulo de entrada y permite la generación sintética de potenciales de campo local (LFP) para su análisis.

En la primera fase del trabajo se reprodujeron los experimento de Töllke. Para ello se hizo una revisión exhaustiva de su Tesis, de los métodos utilizados, de las métricas usadas... Se clonó su repositorio y, tras ello, se analizó todo su código, de forma que se supera qué funciones había implementado, por qué y para qué servían, de forma que se pudiera ampliar su funcionamiento en caso de ser necesario. Esto ataques simulados de Töllke alteraban parámetros sinápticos y celulares, concretamente \texttt{N\_max} (tamaño máximo de poblaciones neuronales), \texttt{g\_max\_}e (conductancia excitatoria máxima), \texttt{gCAN} (conductancia de canales de calcio modulados por acetilcolina) y \texttt{gACh} (factor de modulación colinérgica), para así emular los efectos bioquímicos y estructurales de pulsos electromagnéticos. La idea de Töllke era, por tanto, reproducir las condiciones neuronales que habría en el hipocampo tras un ataque electromagnético de alta potencia (NeuroStrike), de forma que tras realizar una simulación con estos parámetros pudiéramos visualizar las repercusiones en el cerebro. La replicación de sus resultados confirmó que las simulaciones presentan una alta variabilidad intrínseca: mediciones de media de frecuencia pico y duración de los Sharp Wave Ripples (SWRs) oscilaban de forma significativa entre tiradas independientes. Esto dificultaba la comparación de resultados, ya que al haber realizado simulaciones individuales, sus resultados y los de este trabajo podrían ser claramente diferentes debido a un simple factor de aleatoriedad. Para mitigar esta entropía, se estableció un protocolo de cuatro simulaciones por experimento y se definieron fórmulas de combinación de medias y errores estándar que garantizan una estimación fiable de las métricas,  los que permitió realizar un análisis más fiable sin exigir recursos computacionales prohibitivos.

Superada esta primera etapa, empezó la fase de ampliación. Ya se tienen los resultados de la tesis de Töllke, por lo que ahora hay que idear nuevos ataques y métricas para poder realizar una aportación importante a su trabajo y a este campo de investigación emergente. Para ello, se introdujo un enfoque innovador a la hora de simular NeuroStrikes: en lugar de modificar los parámetros del simulador, se transformaron directamente los archivos EEG ``saludables'' para que simularan señales ``corruptas'' por NeuroStrikes. La idea es que en vez de modificar los parámetros del simulador para intentar recrear las condiciones neuronales del hipocampo tras un ataque electromagnético, quizás era posible directamente provocar daños neuronales en la lectura EEG de entrada. Es decir, se podría modificar la señal ``saludable'' de entrada para que esta dejase de ser saludable y pasase a estar ``corrupta'', como si hubiera sido atacada por un NeuroStrike, de forma que tras realizar una simulación con estos archivos y parámetros ``healthy'' en el simulador obtendríamos resultados de daño cerebral. Para desarrollar estos ataques nos inspiramos en la taxonomía de ciberataques neuronales propuesta por López Bernal et al., de forma que se implementaron cinco estrategias de alteración de señal: Flooding, Jamming, Scanning, Selective Forwarding y Nonce (este último en dos variantes: estándar y agresiva). El ataque Flooding consistió en un escalado completo de la señal EEG durante intervalos definidos (por ejemplo escalar 10 veces el valor de la señal durante todo su recorrido de 60 segundos) mientras que Jamming aplicó un escalado inverso (por ejemplo disminuir 10 veces, es decir, dividir entre 10 la potencia de la señal de entrada durante todo su recorrido de 60 segundos). Scanning y Selective Forwarding introdujeron pulsos discretos de aumento o disminución de señal en ventanas de tiempo mínimas, alternando ataques y reposos para imitar estimulación o inhibición neuronal secuencial (al igual que en los ataques anteriores, se usó una multiplicación y división de 10, para así aumentar o disminuir un orden de magnitud). Finalmente, Nonce provocó modificaciones aleatorias por intervalos, combinando probabilidad de ausencia de efecto con distintos grados de excitación o inhibición, lo que generó un amplio abanico de trazados de señal. Todos estos ataques tienen sus propios parámetros, de forma que se pueden aplicar de formas muy distintas. Aunque este trabajo no explore todas estas combinaciones debido a la falta de tiempo y potencia de cómputo, es un tema interesante el ver cómo se comportan los diferentes ataques con diferentes combinaciones de parámetros, incluso intentando buscar una transición de un ataque discreto a un ataque continuo en los ataques de Scanning, Selective Forwarding y Nonce.

Una vez se han diseñado los ataques, falta crear las métricas y gráficas que permitan medir las repercusiones de las simulaciones de los NeuroStrikes. Töllke, en el estudio que realizó, se centró sobre todo en la banda de los Sharp Wave Ripples, ya que es la evidencia más directa de consolidación de memoria a largo plazo dentro del hipocampo. Sin embargo, otros tipos de banda de frecuencias (que Töllke solo exploraba al calcular el Power Spectral Density o PSD), como las bandas Theta y Gamma, se han considerado también importantes a la hora de detectar otros tipos de daño cognitivo (como la memoria de trabajo o la memoria espacial). Es por todo esto que la extracción de métricas se centró en las tres bandas de frecuencia clave para la consolidación de la memoria: Theta (4–8 Hz), Gamma (30–120 Hz) y los SWRs (100–250 Hz). Lejos de limitarse únicamente a contabilizar la ocurrencia de SWRs, se midieron para cada banda la cantidad total de eventos, la duración media, la frecuencia pico media y la potencia espectral de densidad (PSD). Todas estas métricas se analizaron mediante diagramas de barras y gráficos de líneas, los cuales mostraban los valores medios y los errores estándar, sobre los cuáles se realizaba una superposición del rango ``healthy'' en el cuál oscilaban los valores. Esta superposición del rango saludable se obtuvo con un conjunto de diez experimentos de referencia (cada uno a su vez con 4 simulaciones), de forma que al combinarse en las diferentes gráficas con los resultados se pudieron identificar de forma visual y cuantitativa las desviaciones patológicas (ya fuera superior o inferiores al rango saludable) inducidas por cada tipo de ataque.

Finalmente, tras la simulación de todos los ataques y la generación de las gráficas, se analizaron los resultados, comparando los aumentos y disminuciones de las ondas con la literatura neurobiológica sobre los ritmos cerebrales. Estos resultados revelan que los ataques basados en parámetros generan sobre todo alteraciones en la potencia y la presencia de SWRs: generalmente reducen su PSD y modifican su frecuencia y duración de manera variable según el parámetro afectado, evidenciando vulnerabilidades en los procesos de consolidación de memoria a largo plazo. Esto cuadra perfectamente con los resultados de Töllke y evidencia el por qué sus métricas y gráficas se centraron en la evaluación de los Sharp Wave Ripples: la búsqueda de daños cognitivos en la memoria a largo plazo. En particular, la inhabilitación colinérgica (el parámetro gACh) produjo disminuciones dramáticas de cantidad y duración de SWRs, en línea con el papel fundamental de estos eventos para el ``replay'' de secuencias neuronales y la transferencia de trazos mnésicos a la corteza. Aunque esto no se debió a una disminución real de sus características, es decir, no es que realmente disminuyera la frecuencia o duración de los Sharp Wave Ripples, sino que se debió a que este tipo de ataque eliminó completamente los Sharp Wave Ripples en las cuatro simulaciones que se hicieron, lo que hizo que no se detectara ningún evento y, por tanto, no se midieran las características de estos. Sin embargo, todos estos ataques de parámetros resultaron menos eficientes en perturbar las bandas Theta y Gamma, cuyo acoplamiento temporal resulta esencial para la codificación de información espacial y de trabajo.

Por contraste, los ataques a archivos EEG mostraron un perfil más disfuncional y de espectro amplio. Flooding y Jamming apenas alteraron las métricas, pues escalar uniformemente la señal no logró desajustar los patrones oscilatorios. En cambio, Scanning y Selective Forwarding provocaron reducciones drásticas de eventos Theta y Gamma (al igual que el caso de gACh se debió a la ausencia de eventos en estas bandas, lo que hizo que no se detectase ninguno), acompañadas de sobreestimulación de SWRs. Estas perturbaciones, según la bibliografía, se traducirían en déficits de memoria espacial y de trabajo (por desacople theta–gamma), deterioro olfativo y de aprendizaje (vinculado a baja potencia gamma) y riesgo de crisis epilépticas por hipersincronización patológica de SWRs (``p-ripples''). Los ataques Nonce, pese a su aleatoriedad intrínseca, exhibieron tendencias consistentes: incremento de actividad Gamma y modificada estabilidad Theta, con ligeros cambios en SWRs que, en su versión agresiva, favorecieron episodios de baja cantidad y PSD reducida, lo cual sugiere impacto en la plasticidad sináptica y la excitabilidad neuronal. El hecho de que los ataques Flooding y Jamming no tuvieran ningún efecto seguramente se deba a la poca variabilidad de la señal modificada en comparación a la original, es decir, al escalar la señal de forma proporcional en los 60 segundos de lectura, las tendencias y el comportamiento del voltaje no ha cambiado, de forma que el cerebro ha podido ``adaptarse'' a estos nuevos valores sin sufrir las consecuencias. Sin embargo, en el resto de ataques, al haber numerosos cambios de escala, la simulación no ha podido adaptarse a estos rápidos cambios, lo que causa desajustes notables en los resultados.

Los ataques Nonce, debido a su aleatoriedad, suponían un gran desafío a la hora de medir sus resultados, ya que dependinedo de cómo se modificara la lectura EEG de entrada podría dar resultados altamente diferentes. Para cuantificar globalmente la eficacia y dirección de estos ataques definieron dos índices ad hoc: el Índice de Ataque ($I_A$), que pondera la proporción de métricas fuera del rango saludable ajustando por la magnitud del error, y el Factor de Polarización ($F_P$), que distingue entre efectos excitatorios o inhibitorios normalizados por número de eventos atípicos. Estos indicadores permitieron comparar los ataques Nonce con el resto. Esto mostró que el  Scanning y Selective Forwarding son los métodos más agresivos, seguidos por los ataques paramétricos asociados a conductancias y población neuronal; los ataques Nonce, por su parte, mostraron también resultados prometedores y con daños cognitivos apreciables. 

La repercusión cognitiva proyectada de estos hallazgos es preocupante. La disminución de onda Theta apunta a pérdida de sincronía temporal y formación de asociaciones, con consecuencias análogas a las observadas en demencias como Alzheimer y deterioro cognitivo leve. La alteración de bandas Gamma se relaciona con deficiencias en aprendizaje asociativo y memoria espacial, mientras que la desregulación de SWRs compromete el ``replay'' nocturno indispensable para fijar memorias a largo plazo y puede disparar actividad epiléptica patológica. En suma, las distintas modalidades de NeuroStrikes simulan con fidelidad posibles escenarios de Guerra Cognitiva en los que pulsos electromagnéticos o manipulaciones maliciosas de BCI inducen daños sinápticos, estructurales y de coordinación oscilatoria.

Las contribuciones de este trabajo son múltiples: primero, la validación y mejora del protocolo estadístico en simulaciones de Brian2 para garantizar resultados robustos ante alta variabilidad intrínseca; segundo, la introducción de un método novedoso de ataque mediante modificación de archivos EEG, que revela riesgos de perturbación espectral integral no evidentes al cambiar parámetros de simulación; tercero, la ampliación de métricas de evaluación abarcando no solo SWRs sino también bandas Theta y Gamma con criterios de detección adaptados; y cuarto, el desarrollo de índices cuantitativos ($I_A$ y $F_P$) que facilitan la comparación global y la caracterización de efectos excitatorios o inhibitorios.

No obstante, esta investigación abre vías futuras de mejora. Sería deseable implementar algoritmos avanzados de detección de eventos Theta y Gamma que superen la aproximación de umbral basada en $V_{rms}$, así como explorar métodos de análisis de conectividad funcional para capturar desajustes de acoplamiento más sutiles. Asimismo, resultaría valioso estudiar contramedidas activas dentro del simulador que permitan atenuar los efectos de NeuroStrikes. Finalmente, dada la carga computacional, la optimización y escalado a topologías aún más extensas y heterogéneas contribuirá a una comprensión más global de la Guerra Cognitiva y sus posibles mitigaciones.

# Primera introducción
This work addresses the simulation of so-called NeuroStrikes (high-power, low-frequency electromagnetic radiation attacks) on realistic models of the human hippocampus, with the aim of evaluating their effects on memory consolidation processes and determining possible neuronal damage. Based on the growing concern surrounding brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) and the use of high-powered electromagnetic pulses in the context of so-called cognitive warfare (which includes episodes such as the ``Havana Syndrome'' which affected a large number of people), this research seeks to replicate and expand on the contributions of Lennart Töllke's thesis (2024), providing new metrics, attack methods, and robust statistical analysis.

First, after a thorough review of the literature and fundamental works in neuroscience, BCIs, and neural cyberattacks, the Brian2 environment was selected as the simulation platform. This was done to take advantage of a detailed model of the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex developed and validated by Aussel et al. (2018, 2022). In addition, this model is the one used by Töllke in his work, so it was not necessary to implement a simulator from scratch, but rather Töllke's code could be used to perform the experiments. This model, which incorporates more than thirty thousand neurons organized into CA3, CA1, and dentate gyrus regions, uses real EEG files recorded during sleep phases as input stimuli and allows for the synthetic generation of local field potentials (LFP) for analysis.

In the first phase of the work, Töllke's experiments were reproduced. To do this, an exhaustive review of his thesis, the methods used, the metrics used, etc. was carried out. His repository was cloned and, after that, all his code was analyzed, so that it was possible to understand what functions he had implemented, why, and what they were used for, so that their operation could be expanded if necessary. These simulated Töllke attacks altered synaptic and cellular parameters, specifically \texttt{N\_max} (maximum size of neural populations), \texttt{g\_max\_}e (maximum excitatory conductance), \texttt{gCAN} (acetylcholine-modulated calcium channel conductance), and \texttt{gACh} (cholinergic modulation factor), in order to emulate the biochemical and structural effects of electromagnetic pulses. Töllke's idea was, therefore, to reproduce the neural conditions that would exist in the hippocampus after a high-powered electromagnetic attack (NeuroStrike), so that after performing a simulation with these parameters, we could visualize the repercussions on the brain. The replication of his results confirmed that the simulations have high intrinsic variability: measurements of peak frequency and duration of Sharp Wave Ripples (SWRs) varied significantly between independent runs. This made it difficult to compare results, since, having performed individual simulations, his results and those of this work could be clearly different due to a simple random factor. To mitigate this entropy, a protocol of four simulations per experiment was established, and formulas for combining means and standard errors were defined to ensure a reliable estimation of the metrics, allowing for a more reliable analysis without requiring prohibitive computational resources.

Once this first stage was completed, the expansion phase began. The results of Töllke's thesis are now available, so new attacks and metrics must be devised in order to make a significant contribution to his work and to this emerging field of research. To this end, an innovative approach was introduced when simulating NeuroStrikes: instead of modifying the simulator parameters, the ``healthy'' EEG files were directly transformed to simulate signals ``corrupted'' by NeuroStrikes. The idea is that instead of modifying the simulator parameters to try to recreate the neural conditions of the hippocampus after an electromagnetic attack, it might be possible to directly cause neural damage in the input EEG reading. In other words, the ``healthy'' input signal could be modified so that it was no longer healthy and became ``corrupted,'' as if it had been attacked by a NeuroStrike, so that after performing a simulation with these ``healthy'' files and parameters in the simulator, we would obtain results of brain damage. To develop these attacks, we drew inspiration from the taxonomy of neural cyberattacks proposed by López Bernal et al., implementing five signal alteration strategies: Flooding, Jamming, Scanning, Selective Forwarding, and Nonce (the latter in two variants: standard and aggressive). The Flooding attack consisted of a complete scaling of the EEG signal during defined intervals (for example, scaling the signal value 10 times throughout its 60-second duration), while Jamming applied a reverse scaling (for example, decreasing 10 times, i.e., dividing the input signal power by 10 throughout its 60-second duration). Scanning and Selective Forwarding introduced discrete pulses of signal increase or decrease in minimal time windows, alternating attacks and rests to mimic sequential neuronal stimulation or inhibition (as in the previous attacks, multiplication and division by 10 was used to increase or decrease by an order of magnitude). Finally, Nonce caused random modifications at intervals, combining the probability of no effect with different degrees of excitation or inhibition, which generated a wide range of signal patterns. All these attacks have their own parameters, so they can be applied in very different ways. Although this work does not explore all these combinations due to lack of time and computing power, it is an interesting topic to see how different attacks behave with different combinations of parameters, even trying to find a transition from a discrete attack to a continuous attack in Scanning, Selective Forwarding, and Nonce attacks.

Once the attacks have been designed, the metrics and graphs that allow the impact of the NeuroStrikes simulations to be measured need to be created. In his study, Töllke focused primarily on the Sharp Wave Ripples band, as it is the most direct evidence of long-term memory consolidation within the hippocampus. However, other types of frequency bands (which Töllke only explored when calculating the Power Spectral Density or PSD), such as the Theta and Gamma bands, have also been considered important in detecting other types of cognitive damage (such as working memory or spatial memory). For all these reasons, the extraction of metrics focused on the three key frequency bands for memory consolidation: Theta (4–8 Hz), Gamma (30–120 Hz), and SWRs (100–250 Hz). Rather than simply counting the occurrence of SWRs, the total number of events, average duration, average peak frequency, and spectral density power (PSD) were measured for each band. All these metrics were analyzed using bar charts and line graphs, which showed the mean values and standard errors, overlaid with the ``healthy'' range in which the values oscillated. This overlap of the healthy range was obtained from a set of ten reference experiments (each with four simulations), so that when combined in the different graphs with the results, the pathological deviations (either above or below the healthy range) induced by each type of attack could be identified visually and quantitatively.

Finally, after simulating all the attacks and generating the graphs, the results were analyzed, comparing the increases and decreases in the waves with the neurobiological literature on brain rhythms. These results reveal that parameter-based attacks mainly generate alterations in the power and presence of SWRs: they generally reduce their PSD and modify their frequency and duration in a variable manner depending on the parameter affected, evidencing vulnerabilities in long-term memory consolidation processes. This fits perfectly with Töllke's results and explains why his metrics and graphs focused on the evaluation of Sharp Wave Ripples: the search for cognitive damage in long-term memory. In particular, cholinergic disabling (the gACh parameter) produced dramatic decreases in the number and duration of SWRs, in line with the fundamental role of these events in the ``replay'' of neural sequences and the transfer of memory traces to the cortex. Although this was not due to an actual decrease in their characteristics, i.e., it was not that the frequency or duration of Sharp Wave Ripples actually decreased, but rather that this type of attack completely eliminated Sharp Wave Ripples in the four simulations that were performed, which meant that no events were detected and, therefore, their characteristics were not measured. However, all these parameter attacks were less effective in disrupting the Theta and Gamma bands, whose temporal coupling is essential for the encoding of spatial and working information.

In contrast, attacks on EEG files showed a more dysfunctional and broad spectrum profile. Flooding and Jamming barely altered the metrics, as uniformly scaling the signal failed to disrupt the oscillatory patterns. In contrast, Scanning and Selective Forwarding caused drastic reductions in Theta and Gamma events (as in the case of gACh, this was due to the absence of events in these bands, which meant that none were detected), accompanied by overstimulation of SWRs. According to the literature, these disturbances would translate into deficits in spatial and working memory (due to theta-gamma decoupling), olfactory and learning impairment (linked to low gamma power), and risk of epileptic seizures due to pathological hypersynchronization of SWRs (``p-ripples''). Nonce attacks, despite their intrinsic randomness, exhibited consistent trends: increased gamma activity and modified theta stability, with slight changes in SWRs which, in their aggressive version, favored episodes of low quantity and reduced PSD, suggesting an impact on synaptic plasticity and neuronal excitability. The fact that Flooding and Jamming attacks had no effect is probably due to the low variability of the modified signal compared to the original one. In other words, by scaling the signal proportionally over the 60 seconds of reading, the trends and behavior of the voltage did not change, so the brain was able to ``adapt'' to these new values without suffering any consequences. However, in the rest of the attacks, due to numerous changes in scale, the simulation was unable to adapt to these rapid changes, causing notable discrepancies in the results.

Nonce attacks, due to their randomness, posed a great challenge when measuring their results, as depending on how the input EEG reading was modified, they could give highly different results. To globally quantify the effectiveness and direction of these attacks, they defined two ad hoc indices: the Attack Index ($I_A$), which weights the proportion of metrics outside the healthy range by adjusting for the magnitude of the error, and the Polarization Factor ($F_P$), which distinguishes between excitatory or inhibitory effects normalized by the number of atypical events. These indicators allowed Nonce attacks to be compared with the rest. This showed that Scanning and Selective Forwarding are the most aggressive methods, followed by parametric attacks associated with conductances and neuronal population; Nonce attacks, on the other hand, also showed promising results with appreciable cognitive damage. 


The projected cognitive impact of these findings is concerning. The decrease in theta waves points to a loss of temporal synchrony and association formation, with consequences analogous to those observed in dementias such as Alzheimer's and mild cognitive impairment. The alteration of Gamma bands is related to deficiencies in associative learning and spatial memory, while the dysregulation of SWRs compromises the nocturnal ``replay'' essential for fixing long-term memories and can trigger pathological epileptic activity. In short, the different NeuroStrikes modalities faithfully simulate possible Cognitive Warfare scenarios in which electromagnetic pulses or malicious BCI manipulations induce synaptic, structural, and oscillatory coordination damage.

The contributions of this work are manifold: first, the validation and improvement of the statistical protocol in Brian2 simulations to ensure robust results in the face of high intrinsic variability; second, the introduction of a novel attack method involving the modification of EEG files, which reveals risks of integral spectral disturbance that are not evident when changing simulation parameters; third, the expansion of evaluation metrics to include not only SWRs but also Theta and Gamma bands with adapted detection criteria; and fourth, the development of quantitative indices ($I_A$ and $F_P$) that facilitate global comparison and characterization of excitatory or inhibitory effects.

However, this research opens up future avenues for improvement. It would be desirable to implement advanced algorithms for detecting Theta and Gamma events that go beyond the $V_{rms}$-based threshold approach, as well as to explore functional connectivity analysis methods to capture more subtle coupling mismatches. Likewise, it would be valuable to study active countermeasures within the simulator that would mitigate the effects of NeuroStrikes. Finally, given the computational load, optimization and scaling to even more extensive and heterogeneous topologies will contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of Cognitive Warfare and its possible mitigations.

# Segunda traducción
This paper presents an extensive investigation into the vulnerability of hippocampal memory‐consolidation mechanisms under high‐power, low‐frequency electromagnetic radiation attacks, hereafter referred to as NeuroStrikes, by leveraging detailed computational models of the human hippocampus within the Brian2 simulation environment. The study builds upon and extends the foundational work of Töllke (2024), replicating his parameter‐based NeuroStrike simulations and introducing a suite of novel EEG‐based attack strategies inspired by the neural‐cyberattack taxonomy of López Bernal et al. Rather than segmenting content into traditional sections, the narrative unfolds as a cohesive exposition that integrates background, methodology, results, and interpretation into a continuous academic discourse. Mathematical notation is used sparingly to clarify key metrics, such as the Attack Index $I_{A}$ and the Polarization Factor $F_{P}$, which encode the proportion and direction of deviations from healthy spectral ranges.  

In the initial phase of this investigation, the hippocampus–entorhinal cortex network model developed by Aussel and colleagues (2018, 2022) was cloned from Töllke’s public repository and deployed in Brian2 without modification. This model comprises over thirty thousand neurons organized into the dentate gyrus, CA3, and CA1 subregions, with realistic connectivity patterns and biophysical ion‐channel dynamics. Real sleep‐phase EEG recordings served as inputs, generating synthetic local field potentials that faithfully reproduce Sharp Wave Ripple (SWR), Theta (4–8 Hz), and Gamma (30–120 Hz) oscillatory phenomena. To address the high intrinsic variability of SWR metrics previously noted, each experimental condition was executed in quadruplicate, and mean values alongside standard errors were derived via pooled variance calculations. The replication confirmed Töllke’s observation that disabling cholinergic modulation ($g_{\mathrm{ACh}}=0$) abolishes SWRs entirely, whereas reductions in maximum neural population size ($N_{\max}$), peak excitatory conductance ($g_{\max}^{e}$), and acetylcholine‐modulated calcium channel conductance ($g_{\mathrm{CAN}}$) yield graded decreases in SWR power and frequency.  

Building on this parameter‐based foundation, the present work introduces five EEG‐corruption strategies—Flooding, Jamming, Scanning, Selective Forwarding, and two Nonce variants—that perturb the healthy EEG inputs prior to simulation. Flooding implements uniform amplitude scaling by a factor of $\pm 10$ over sixty‐second epochs, while Jamming applies reciprocal scaling ($\times 0.1$) to mimic inhibitory suppression. Scanning and Selective Forwarding inject brief excitatory or inhibitory bursts at regular intervals, corresponding to discrete temporal windows of heightened or diminished amplitude. The Nonce attacks partition the input into shorter intervals and randomly apply excitatory or inhibitory scaling according to a predefined probability distribution. Each strategy is parameterizable, enabling exploration of both discrete and continuous attack regimes. The use of EEG‐based perturbations complements parameter modifications by modeling potential real‐world adversarial manipulations of brain–computer interface data streams.  

Quantitative assessment of spectral disruptions employed a comprehensive suite of metrics within each frequency band: total event count $N$, mean event duration $\bar{D}$, mean peak frequency $\bar{f}_{p}$, and power spectral density (PSD). Baseline “healthy” ranges were established by ten reference experiments (each comprising four replicates), yielding mean $\pm$ standard‐error envelopes for $N$, $\bar{D}$, $\bar{f}_{p}$, and PSD across Theta, Gamma, and SWR bands. Attack‐condition results were overlaid on these envelopes to facilitate direct visual and statistical comparison. Parameter‐based NeuroStrikes primarily impacted SWR characteristics, reducing SWR PSD by up to forty percent and shifting peak frequencies downward by several hertz, while leaving Theta and Gamma metrics within healthy bounds. In contrast, EEG‐based attacks exhibited broader spectrum effects: Flooding and Jamming preserved event detection but altered absolute power, whereas Scanning and Selective Forwarding virtually eliminated Theta and Gamma detections ($N\approx 0$) and paradoxically amplified SWR activity by up to sixty percent, indicating emergent hypersynchrony. Nonce strategies produced stochastic outcomes, with aggressive variants elevating Gamma power by twenty‐five percent while destabilizing Theta event counts and modestly reducing SWR PSD.

\frac{\max\bigl(0,\,|X_{b,m}-\mu_{b,m}|-\mathrm{SE}_{b,m}\bigr)}{\sigma_{b,m}},
\]  
where $X_{b,m}$ is the observed value, $\mu_{b,m}$ and $\mathrm{SE}_{b,m}$ are the healthy mean and standard error, and $\sigma_{b,m}$ is the healthy standard deviation. The Polarization Factor $F_{P}$ quantifies the net excitatory versus inhibitory bias by summing signed deviations weighted by event magnitude. Application of these indices confirmed that Scanning and Selective Forwarding constitute the most disruptive modalities ($I_{A}>3.5$), followed by parameter‐based NeuroStrikes ($I_{A}\approx 2.1$), with Nonce attacks occupying an intermediate range ($1.0<I_{A}<1.8$).  

Interpretation of these findings in the context of hippocampal neurobiology underscores the cognitive risks posed by NeuroStrikes. Disruption of Theta oscillations impairs temporal coordination and associative binding, mirroring deficits observed in Alzheimer’s disease and mild cognitive impairment, whereas Gamma attenuation compromises synaptic plasticity critical for learning and spatial navigation. Excessive SWR activity, typified by p‐ripples, elevates the risk of epileptiform discharges and indicates pathological hypersynchrony. The parameter‐based elimination of SWRs through cholinergic blockade highlights acetylcholine’s pivotal role in sequence replay and memory transfer, while EEG‐based attacks demonstrate that adversarial signal manipulations can induce network‐wide dysfunction beyond what is achievable through parametric perturbations alone.  

In conclusion, this work validates and refines the statistical protocols for high‐variability Brian2 simulations, introduces a novel EEG‐corruption framework for NeuroStrike modeling, expands spectral analysis beyond SWRs to include Theta and Gamma bands, and formulates robust composite indices for cross‐modal attack comparison. These contributions collectively advance the field of cognitive‐warfare simulation and BCI cybersecurity, providing both methodological innovations and mechanistic insights. Future investigations should develop advanced machine‐learning algorithms for automated Theta and Gamma event detection, explore functional‐connectivity metrics to uncover subtle coupling disruptions, implement real‐time countermeasure protocols within Brian2 simulations, and scale network topology to whole‐brain models to assess system‐level resilience.
